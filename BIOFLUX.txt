BioFlux Array v1.0 — Complete Project Description
Full Title: BioFlux Array: Dual-Shoe Electromagnetic Energy Harvesting Microgrid with Hybrid Storage and Real-Time Wireless Power Analytics

What This Project Is
BioFlux Array is a wearable energy-harvesting prototype that converts the repetitive mechanical energy of walking, running, and jumping into usable electrical energy. The core concept places a compact electromagnetic kinetic generator inside the heel region of each shoe. Each shoe functions as a fully independent energy node — its own harvester, its own power conditioning circuit, its own storage system, and its own ESP32-S3 microcontroller with BLE and Wi-Fi wireless transmission. There is no connection between the two shoes. The left shoe operates as BioFlux-L and the right shoe as BioFlux-R, each broadcasting real-time energy data to a phone or browser dashboard simultaneously.
The system is designed as a wearable microgrid — not simply a generator attached to a battery. Each node harvests energy, conditions it, stores it in a hybrid supercapacitor-plus-lithium-ion architecture, regulates it to a clean 3.3V rail, and then uses that rail to power a sensing and telemetry layer that measures and broadcasts exactly what the system is doing in real time. This makes the project educational, physically demonstrable, and analytically rigorous — the supercapacitor charging curve alone constitutes hard physical proof of energy harvesting that cannot be faked.

Mechanical Harvester — Structure and Working
Inside the heel cavity of each shoe, a compression plate made from 1.5mm thick 6061-T6 aluminum spans the full heel width. When the user steps, the heel deflects 6–10mm vertically. This compression is transmitted to a spring-return system using two 65Mn spring steel compression springs rated at 20 N/mm each, which both absorb shock and return the plate to its original position between steps.
The linear compression is converted to rotational motion by a rack-and-pinion mechanism. The rack is a 150mm segment of M0.8 module case-hardened steel (EN36 / 20MnCr5), surface hardness 58–62 HRC with a tough 30–40 HRC core. M0.8 module means each tooth has a 2.513mm pitch — fine enough to fit the heel cavity but strong enough to survive repeated impact. The pinion is a 14-tooth M0.8 brass gear (CuZn37), which acts as the intentional wear element — it wears preferentially over the rack, so only the pinion needs replacing over time. A second spur gear amplification stage uses a 10-tooth steel driver meshing with an 80-tooth steel driven gear, achieving a total gear ratio of approximately 1:35 to 1:50 from heel motion to generator shaft.
All shafts are 3mm diameter precision ground silver steel (EN8), mounted in 623ZZ sealed miniature ball bearings (3×10×4mm, two per shaft) pressed into the 6061-T6 aluminum housing. Sealed bearings are non-negotiable — open bearings in a shoe fill with sweat and debris within days. A brass flywheel, 25mm OD and 4mm thick (approximately 16–18g), is pressed onto the generator shaft to sustain rotation between heel strikes, smoothing the intermittent input into a more continuous electrical output.
The generator is a coreless DC motor (820 or 1020 series, RC hobby grade) running in reverse. Coreless motors have near-zero cogging torque, meaning the heel compression force does not have to overcome magnetic detent to start spinning — critical at the low force levels available from human walking. The motor is rated 6–12V at target RPM range and specified with sealed ball bearings internally.

Shock Absorption — Handling Running and Jumping Forces
Walking delivers approximately 900N peak heel force. Running delivers 2,000–2,500N. Jumping and landing delivers up to 5,000N. M0.8 steel rack tooth shear occurs at approximately 1,200N under point loading. This means raw jump forces would destroy the gear train on the first impact without a protection system.
Three layers of protection reduce the transmitted force. First, a 10mm EVA foam pad (same material as running shoe midsoles) sits between the outsole strike surface and the aluminum compression plate — this extends impact duration from 20ms to approximately 40ms and reduces peak force by ~35%. Second, the two compression springs absorb and return energy elastically, reducing transmitted force by a further ~25%. Third, a machined hard stop in the aluminum housing limits total rack travel to 10mm regardless of input force — this prevents mechanism over-travel on extreme landings. Together, these three layers reduce a 5,000N jump landing to approximately 1,000–1,100N transmitted to the rack teeth — within the survival limit of M0.8 steel gears.
The entire mechanism sits in a 6061-T6 aluminum housing (55mm × 40mm × 18mm), CNC-machined or hand-filed from flat stock. The housing is bonded to the shoe heel cavity using Araldite two-part structural epoxy and secured with M2 stainless machine screws threaded into aluminum inserts in the midsole.

Electronics — Per-Shoe Independent PCB
Each shoe carries one complete custom 2-layer PCB, approximately 60mm × 40mm, fabricated through JLCPCB. The PCB is organized into functional sections.
The generator output (irregular AC or variable-polarity DC) connects via a 2-pin JST connector to an MB6S surface-mount bridge rectifier, which performs full-wave rectification. A 47µF electrolytic bulk capacitor smooths the rectified waveform. A 1N5819 Schottky diode (Vf = 0.3V) performs OR-ing isolation — it allows the generator to feed the energy bus but prevents reverse current from draining back into the generator.
The common energy bus feeds a 3F / 2.7V supercapacitor in parallel, which acts as the fast burst storage element. The supercapacitor charges almost instantaneously from each heel strike and is the primary demonstration component — its charging curve directly visualizes energy harvesting activity. An 18650 Li-ion cell (Samsung or LG, 2,500–3,500 mAh, 3.7V nominal) provides long-duration storage and keeps the ESP32-S3 alive during low-harvest phases. The TP4056 module manages safe CC/CV charging of the Li-ion cell.
An AP2112K-3.3 low-dropout regulator (Iq < 55µA) converts the battery voltage to a clean 3.3V rail. Six 100nF ceramic decoupling capacitors are placed within 1–2mm of every IC power pin. Without these, switching noise from the rectifier corrupts ADC readings and causes random ESP32 resets.
Three voltage divider networks (10kΩ / 6.8kΩ resistor pairs) scale the bus voltage, supercapacitor voltage, and battery voltage into the 0–3.3V ADC input range of the ESP32-S3. An optional INA219 current monitor IC on the I2C bus (SDA GPIO8, SCL GPIO9) enables direct P = V × I power measurement. The entire PCB is spray-coated with acrylic conformal coating after assembly to protect solder joints from vibration fatigue and sweat ingress.
The ESP32-S3-WROOM-1 reads all ADC channels every 100–500ms, averages 16 samples per reading to reduce noise, computes supercapacitor stored energy using E = ½CV², estimates instantaneous power, tracks cumulative session energy, and classifies activity state (idle, walking, running) based on bus voltage thresholds. It transmits BLE 5.0 advertisement packets every 5–10 seconds containing all measured parameters. In deep sleep between transmissions, it draws approximately 7µA — enabling duty-cycled operation that reduces effective consumption to 10–15mW, within the realistic harvest range.

Realistic Energy Output
Under a real v1 student-built prototype, the overall system efficiency from heel strike mechanical energy to stored electrical energy is approximately 8–15%. This accounts for gear train friction losses (~45%), generator cogging and winding resistance losses (~25%), rectifier diode drops (~10%), and regulator quiescent draw (~4%). The resulting output is 1–5mW average per shoe during walking — not the 30mW figures cited in optimistic research literature. Over a 6-hour active session of walking and light running, both shoes combined realistically produce 18–90 kJ of electrical energy. This is sufficient to power the ESP32-S3 in duty-cycled BLE mode (which needs approximately 10–15mW), visibly charge the supercapacitor across multiple sessions, and trickle-charge the Li-ion backup battery over days of use. The supercapacitor charging curve — voltage rising measurably from 0V to 1.5–2.0V over a 15–30 minute walking session — is the primary demonstration evidence presented to judges.

Custom App and Dashboard
Each shoe broadcasts as a separate BLE device. A custom mobile app (React Native or Flutter) or browser-based web dashboard (ESP32 Wi-Fi in access point mode) connects to both BioFlux-L and BioFlux-R simultaneously and merges the data streams client-side. The live dashboard displays: generator bus voltage per shoe, supercapacitor voltage per shoe, battery voltage per shoe, estimated instantaneous power, system mode (idle / harvesting / charging / transmitting), session total energy generated, peak power reached, average power over time, and step-count estimation based on bus voltage pulse frequency. Session data is timestamped and can be exported as CSV for post-session analysis. The comparative left-versus-right shoe output display is a unique data product that no prior shoe harvester project has demonstrated — it reveals gait asymmetry as a measurable energy differential between shoes.

Bill of Materials and Total Cost
Per shoe, the mechanical components — M0.8 steel rack and gears, coreless motor, brass flywheel, springs, bearings, silver steel shafts, CNC aluminum housing, EVA foam, hardware, and epoxy — cost ₹2,340–₹5,120. The electronics per shoe — ESP32-S3 module, rectifier, supercapacitor, 18650 cell, TP4056, regulator, INA219, resistors, capacitors, PCB fabrication, connectors, silicone wire, and conformal coat — cost ₹1,515–₹3,130. Across both shoes plus tools and consumables, the total project cost is ₹8,210–₹17,700, with a realistic mid-quality build landing at approximately ₹10,000–₹12,000 if common components like the Li-ion cell, resistors, and capacitors are already on hand.

Summary
BioFlux Array v1.0 is a physically built, analytically instrumented, wirelessly connected wearable energy microgrid — not a simulation or concept. It demonstrates electromagnetic kinetic energy harvesting through a precision M0.8 steel gear train with layered shock protection, dual independent ESP32-S3 nodes with BLE telemetry, hybrid supercapacitor-plus-Li-ion storage, and a real-time dual-shoe comparative dashboard. Its honest output of 1–5mW per shoe is presented with full loss-stack analysis and supercapacitor charging curve evidence, making it credible to both science fair judges and university engineering admissions reviewers.
